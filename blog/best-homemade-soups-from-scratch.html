Best Homemade Soups to Make From Scratch This Week

July 10, 2026 · 7 min read

There's a version of soup that comes from a can and tastes like it. And there's a version that fills the whole house with the smell of something actually being cooked — something rich and layered and deeply satisfying in a way that a 99-cent can simply cannot replicate.

The gap between those two things is smaller than you think. Homemade soup from scratch doesn't require professional technique or exotic ingredients. It requires understanding a few principles that every great soup shares — and then applying them to whatever you have in your kitchen.

The Four Principles of Great Soup

1. Build a real base

Every excellent soup starts the same way: aromatics in fat over medium heat, cooked until softened and fragrant. Onion, garlic, celery, carrot — some combination of these, sautéed in olive oil or butter for 5-8 minutes before anything else goes in. This step is non-negotiable. It's the difference between a soup that tastes like hot water with stuff in it and one that tastes like it was built with intention.

2. Toast your spices

Add dried spices directly to the softened aromatics and let them cook in the fat for 60-90 seconds before adding any liquid. This blooms the spices — releasing their oil-soluble compounds and dramatically deepening their flavor. It's a 90-second step that makes a 30% difference in the final result.

3. Use quality broth — or make it from scraps

Your broth is the backbone of the soup. If you're buying it, get low-sodium so you can control the salt. Better yet, save vegetable scraps and chicken bones in a freezer bag and make your own every few weeks. Homemade stock adds a depth that no carton can match.

4. Season at the end

Salt early to build flavor, but taste and adjust at the very end — after everything has simmered and concentrated. Acid is often the missing element in soups that taste "flat": a squeeze of lemon, a splash of vinegar, or a spoonful of tomato paste added at the end can transform a good soup into a great one.

The Best Soups to Make This Week

🍗

Classic Chicken Noodle From Scratch

⏱ 60 min 👨‍👩‍👧‍👦 Family favorite ❄️ Freezes well

The benchmark. Bone-in chicken thighs simmered until falling off the bone, pulled and shredded, returned to a broth built from the drippings with mirepoix, thyme, and bay leaf. Egg noodles added at the end. This is comfort food in its most honest form — and it's dramatically better made from scratch than anything in a can.

🍅

Roasted Tomato Basil Soup

⏱ 50 min 🌱 Vegetarian ❄️ Freezes well

Roasting the tomatoes first — with olive oil, garlic, and onion at high heat — caramelizes their sugars and concentrates their flavor in a way that raw tomatoes simply can't achieve. Blend with warm broth and fresh basil, finish with a swirl of cream or a drizzle of good olive oil. Serve with crusty bread and this becomes a complete meal.

🫘

White Bean, Kale & Sausage Soup

⏱ 45 min 💪 High protein ❄️ Freezes well

One of the most satisfying one-pot meals in the repertoire. Italian sausage broken into pieces and browned, white beans from a can (or dried, soaked overnight), kale added at the end and barely wilted. Finished with parmesan rind simmered in the broth the whole time — this is the secret move that gives the broth an extraordinary silkiness.

🥕

Red Lentil & Coconut Curry Soup

⏱ 40 min 🌱 Vegan ⚡ Quick

Red lentils dissolve into the broth as they cook, creating a naturally thick, creamy consistency without any blending required. Coconut milk adds richness, curry powder and cumin add warmth, and a squeeze of lime at the end brightens everything. This soup is ready in 40 minutes and costs almost nothing to make from pantry staples.

🧅

Slow-Caramelized French Onion Soup

⏱ 90 min 🏆 Impressive 🧀 Gruyère finish

The one soup worth a longer time investment. Onions caramelized low and slow for 45-60 minutes until they collapse into a sweet, jammy, deeply golden mass — this is the foundation of a French onion soup that bears no resemblance to the pale imitations served at chain restaurants. Ladle into oven-safe bowls, float a toasted baguette slice, bury under gruyère, and broil until bubbling.

Big Batch Strategy: Cook Once, Eat All Week

Soup is one of the most efficient foods you can make from a time-per-meal perspective. A pot of chicken noodle that takes an hour produces 8-10 servings. That's a dollar per serving in protein cost, under 10 minutes of active cooking time per meal, and lunch or dinner handled for the next three days.

The most efficient soups for big-batch cooking:

  • Bean-based soups — white bean, black bean, lentil — improve dramatically after a day in the fridge as flavors meld
  • Broth-based soups — chicken noodle, minestrone, vegetable — hold well for 4-5 days refrigerated
  • Pureed soups — tomato, butternut squash, carrot ginger — freeze in individual portions for instant weeknight meals

The rule: make more than you need on Sunday. Your future self — standing in the kitchen at 6pm on Wednesday — will thank you.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best homemade soup to make from scratch?
It depends on what you have, but universally crowd-pleasing options include classic chicken noodle, roasted tomato basil, white bean and kale, and red lentil curry. Start by building a proper base — sautéed aromatics and toasted spices make the biggest difference.
How long does it take to make soup from scratch?
Most homemade soups take 45 minutes to 1.5 hours. Simple vegetable soups can be ready in 30-45 minutes. Heartier soups with dried beans or slow-cooked proteins may take longer. Pressure cooker versions cut most soups to 20-30 minutes.
What are the best soup ingredients to keep in the pantry?
Canned diced tomatoes, canned white beans and chickpeas, broth, dried lentils, coconut milk, and a spice collection including cumin, smoked paprika, and turmeric. With these and any fresh produce or protein, you can build a full soup from scratch.
Can I make a big batch of soup and freeze it?
Yes — most soups freeze beautifully. Best for freezing: broth-based soups, bean soups, and pureed soups. Avoid freezing soups with cream, potatoes, or pasta already added — add those fresh when reheating. Store for up to 3 months.
